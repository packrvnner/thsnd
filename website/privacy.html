Privacy Policy

Thousand iOS application · effective July 3, 2026

The short version

The Thousand app collects no personal data. It has no accounts, no analytics, no advertising, no tracking, and no server of ours that receives anything about you.

What the app stores on your device

If you watch an address or connect a wallet, that address — and your interface preferences — are stored locally on your device only. Deleting the app deletes them. WalletConnect session keys are stored in your device keychain and never leave it.

What leaves your device

The app is a client for public blockchain infrastructure. To display data it sends standard network requests to:

  • Public Base RPC endpoints (mainnet.base.org, publicnode.com, llamarpc.com) — read queries that may include the address you watch or connect. These are queries against public-ledger data.
  • Blockscout (base.blockscout.com) — historical event queries for the burn and lock charts.
  • thsnd.xyz — a static data feed for the Milli page. Our host does not log identifying information beyond ordinary web-server operation.
  • Reown (WalletConnect) relay — only if you connect a wallet. Session traffic is end-to-end encrypted; the relay routes messages between the app and your wallet without reading them. See Reown's privacy policy.

These third parties receive your IP address as a technical consequence of any internet request. We do not control, receive, or combine that information.

What the app never does

  • Never holds or transmits private keys, seed phrases, or funds.
  • Never fingerprints your device or tracks you across apps or sites.
  • Never sells, shares, or monetizes data — there is nothing to sell.

The blockchain itself

Transactions you sign in your own wallet are recorded permanently on the public Base network. That is the nature of a public ledger, not a data practice of this app — nothing published on-chain can be deleted by us or anyone.

Children

The app is not directed at children and is inappropriate for anyone not legally permitted to use digital-asset software in their jurisdiction.
